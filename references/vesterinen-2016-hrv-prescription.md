## What we use this for

The cleanest single RCT supporting HRV-guided training. Underpins the readiness traffic light's specific behavior: when HRV says "rest," skipping the quality session is *probably* the higher-performance choice as well as the lower-injury one.

## Abstract

Measures of heart-rate variability (HRV) have shown potential for use in endurance training prescription. This 8-week study investigated whether HRV-guided timing of moderate- and high-intensity sessions outperformed a traditional predetermined plan in recreational endurance runners.

## Methods

- 40 recreational endurance runners.
- Randomized to two groups for 8 weeks of intensive training:
  - **EXP (HRV-guided)**: timing of moderate/high-intensity sessions determined by morning HRV.
  - **TRAD (Traditional)**: predefined plan with 2–3 vigorous sessions per week.

## Key results

| Metric | HRV-guided (EXP) | Traditional (TRAD) |
|---|---|---|
| Moderate/high-intensity sessions performed | **13.2 ± 6.0** | 17.7 ± 2.5 |
| 3000 m time improvement | **2.1% ± 2.0% (p = 0.004)** | 1.1% ± 2.7% (p = 0.118) |
| VO₂max improvement | 3.7% ± 4.6% | 5.0% ± 5.2% |

The HRV-guided group improved 3000 m performance significantly while doing ~25% fewer hard sessions.

## Conclusion

> "The results suggest the potential of resting HRV to prescribe endurance training by individualizing the timing of vigorous training sessions."

## How this constrains our claims

- ✓ "HRV-guided can deliver better performance with less hard work" — this paper, on n=40.
- Caveat: n=40, 8 weeks, recreational runners 18–45. Generalization to a 47-year-old reconditioning from couch is reasonable inference but not proven.
- The Manresa-Rocamora 2021 meta-analysis aggregating across many such trials found the performance advantage non-significant overall — keep that humility.
