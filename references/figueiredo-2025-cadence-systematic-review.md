## What we use this for

The single strongest evidence behind the dashboard's cadence rule (≥170 spm) and the "cadence is the highest-leverage knee-protective intervention" claim in `principles.md` §7. Note: most of the load-numbers come from primary studies *cited inside* this review (Heiderscheit 2011, Nijs 2020, Peterson 2024) — this review synthesises them.

## Abstract

Running is associated with a 56% incidence of lower extremity injuries in recreational runners. PRISMA review, three databases (2009–2025), 18 included studies. Finding: a moderate cadence increase (typically 5–10% above spontaneous) consistently reduces vertical ground reaction forces, lowers loading rates, shortens stride length, and improves lower limb alignment — *without* compromising running economy. Evidence suggests preventive effects on patellofemoral pain and tibial stress fractures. Cadence retraining is concluded to be "a low-cost, accessible, and effective strategy" for injury reduction.

## Methods

- PRISMA-compliant systematic review.
- PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, 2009 through March 2025.
- 18 studies (randomized, quasi-experimental, cross-sectional, systematic).
- Adults, recreational or competitive runners, cadence as primary variable.
- Quality appraisal: NIH (experimental), AXIS (cross-sectional), Newcastle-Ottawa (cohort), PEDro (RCT), AMSTAR 2 (systematic).
- Narrative synthesis — heterogeneity precluded meta-analysis.

## Key findings on cadence and knee load

### Impact / joint load (synthesizing primary studies)

- **Heiderscheit et al. 2011**: +5% cadence significantly reduces hip and knee loading (lower thigh adduction moment, lower impact force); +10% accentuates the reduction.
- **Nijs et al. 2020**: +7.5% and +15% cadence each significantly reduces impact force and vertical load rate "in all runners".
- **Lenhart et al. 2014**: increased cadence significantly reduces joint loads in the knee and hip.

### Knee valgus and alignment

- **Peterson et al. 2024**: +10% cadence reduces dynamic knee valgus angle by ~2°, with concurrent reduction in hip adduction during support phase.

### Stride and ground contact

Across studies: increased cadence → shorter stride length, reduced ground contact time, reduced vertical oscillation.

## Injury prevention findings

- **Luedke et al. 2016**: runners with cadence ≤166 spm had **6–7× higher tibial-injury risk** vs runners ≥178 spm.
- **Chan et al. 2018**: 62% reduction in injury risk at 12 months in novice runners following gait retraining incorporating cadence modification.
- Patellofemoral pain: cadence increases reduce patellofemoral pressure — suggesting benefit in prevention/treatment of PFPS.

## Running economy

- "Cadence modification did not negatively affect metabolic cost."
- **Van Hooren et al. 2024**: moderate cadence increases showed *positive* impact on running economy.

## Adherence

- Auditory cueing (music) was feasible and effective.
- Without continued feedback, partial regression to baseline was reported.
- Structured follow-up essential.

## Conclusion

A 5–10% increase above spontaneous cadence produces beneficial biomechanical changes without compromising energy efficiency. Evidence supports protective effects against patellofemoral pain and tibial stress conditions. Limitations: small samples, short follow-up, predominance of surrogate biomechanical outcomes over long-term clinical endpoints.

## How this constrains our claims

- ✓ Defensible: "5–10% above spontaneous cadence reduces peak knee load by ~20% (Heiderscheit, Nijs)" — primary studies inside this review.
- ✓ Defensible: "low cadence (<166 spm) is associated with 6–7× tibial injury risk" — Luedke 2016, cited in review.
